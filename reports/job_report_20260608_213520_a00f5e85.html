Q: What is the difference between supervised and unsupervised learning in the context of NLP?
Difficulty: easy
Answer guide: Supervised learning involves training a model on labeled data, while unsupervised learning involves training a model on unlabeled data. In NLP, supervised learning is often used for tasks like text classification, while unsupervised learning is used for tasks like clustering or dimensionality reduction.
Q: How does tokenization work in NLP, and what are some common tokenization techniques?
Difficulty: medium
Answer guide: Tokenization is the process of breaking down text into individual words or tokens. Common tokenization techniques include word-level tokenization, subword tokenization, and character-level tokenization. Word-level tokenization splits text into individual words, while subword tokenization splits words into subwords or word pieces.
Q: What is the purpose of embedding in NLP, and how does it work?
Difficulty: medium
Answer guide: Embedding in NLP is a technique used to represent words or tokens as dense vectors in a high-dimensional space. This allows words with similar meanings to be mapped to nearby points in the vector space, enabling the model to capture semantic relationships between words.
Q: How does the FAISS library work, and what are its advantages in NLP applications?
Difficulty: medium
Answer guide: FAISS is a library for efficient similarity search and clustering of dense vectors. It works by indexing the vectors and using algorithms like k-means or hierarchical clustering to efficiently search for similar vectors. FAISS is often used in NLP applications for tasks like semantic search or text clustering.
Q: What is the difference between a language model and a language generator, and how do they work?
Difficulty: hard
Answer guide: A language model is a statistical model that predicts the next word in a sequence of words, given the context of the previous words. A language generator, on the other hand, is a model that generates text based on a given prompt or topic. Language generators often use language models as a component to generate coherent and context-dependent text.
